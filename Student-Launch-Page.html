World History

Pre-Shoah Jewish Communities

Research a vibrant Jewish community as it existed before the Holocaust. Understand the people, culture, and institutions that made it unique.

What Is This Project?

A focused, 4-period research project on pre-Holocaust Jewish life.

The Big Idea

Jewish history is often taught through tragedy. In this project, you research what a Jewish community actually looked like before the Holocaust: its synagogues, schools, leaders, daily life, and culture. The goal is to understand these communities as living, complex societies.

How It Works (4 Class Periods)

  • Period 1 (before break): Form groups of 4, pick your community, assign roles, and start researching using provided sources.
  • Period 2 (after break): Finish research, combine your work, and build a short slide deck (7 slides).
  • Periods 3-4 (after break): Group presentations (5 minutes each) followed by quick Q&A from the teacher.

What You Submit (on presentation day)

  1. Intake Form -- your teacher will share a Google Form. One person submits it for your group during Period 1.
  2. Research Worksheet -- one per student, filled out during Periods 1-2
  3. Presentation Slides -- one deck per group (7 slides)
Time is tight.

You have 4 periods total. Use the Source Bank tab to go directly to real sources instead of wandering the internet. The research worksheet guides exactly what to look for. Stay focused and you will finish on time.

Slides & Q&A

How to build your deck and prepare for questions.

Slide Structure (7 slides)

  1. Title -- Community name, location, group members
  2. Overview -- Where, how large, when
  3. Historical Context -- Political, economic, major events
  4. Daily Life -- Religious life, schools, organizations, culture
  5. Key Figures -- Important people and why they mattered
  6. Map & Visuals -- At least one map + a historical photo
  7. Conclusion -- What made this community unique

Slide Rules

  • Max 5 lines of text per slide. If you have more, cut it.
  • Font size: 24pt minimum for body text.
  • Do not read your slides. Slides are visual support. You speak from knowledge.
  • Consistent design. Same font, same colors, same layout. The Visual Specialist checks this.
  • Include at least 1 map and 1 photo. The Source Bank has links to image archives.

Q&A Prep (2-3 questions per group)

After your 5-minute presentation, the teacher will ask 2-3 quick questions. These are not meant to trick you. They confirm you understand what you researched. Questions may include:

  • What was this community known for?
  • Why was it located where it was?
  • What institutions were most important to Jewish life there?
  • Who was an important figure and why?
  • What surprised you about this community?
  • How was it different from other Jewish communities?

Best prep: Write a 3-sentence summary of your research from memory. If you can do that, you are ready.

AI Policy

Honest rules for using AI on this project.

The Bottom Line

AI is a tool. You can use it. But the Q&A after your presentation is worth 30 points, and no tool can answer for you. If you do not understand what is on your slides, it will be obvious in about 15 seconds of questioning.

Go Ahead

  • Use AI to make your slides look good (design, layout, formatting)
  • Ask AI to explain a term or concept you do not understand
  • Use AI to brainstorm research questions or organize your notes
  • Ask AI to help you find sources (then go read the actual source yourself)
  • Use AI to clean up grammar or spelling on text you already wrote

Do Your Own Thinking

  • Your Research Worksheet answers should come from reading your sources, not from asking AI to summarize them for you
  • The content on your slides (the actual historical information) should come from your research, not from a prompt
  • Your conclusions about the community should be yours

The difference: AI can help you present your work. It should not do your work.

Why this matters:

After your presentation, the teacher will ask you questions about your research. You will answer in your own words, on the spot. That is 30 of your 100 points. Students who did their own research will handle this easily. Students who had AI do the thinking for them will not.
